Complete Property Buying Guide for New Zealand

Updated November 2024: This guide reflects current New Zealand property regulations including the July 2024 LVR changes and DTI (Debt-to-Income) ratio restrictions. Always verify current requirements with your mortgage broker or bank as regulations may change.

Step 1: Assess Your Financial Position

Understanding Deposit Requirements (2024)

Standard Requirements:

  • Owner-Occupiers: Generally require a 20% deposit to avoid mortgage insurance and secure better interest rates
  • First Home Buyers (Standard): Can sometimes access mortgages with 10% deposit, though this typically requires mortgage insurance
  • First Home Loan Programme: Eligible buyers can purchase with just 5% deposit, with Kāinga Ora underwriting the loan
  • Investment Properties: Typically require 35-40% deposit under current LVR restrictions

Important Context: As of July 2024, LVR restrictions have been eased to help first home buyers enter the market. Banks are now allowed to allocate 20% of their lending to 'high LVR owner occupiers' who don't meet the standard 20% deposit requirement.

Debt-to-Income (DTI) Ratios

Introduced in July 2024, DTI restrictions cap total debt at six times annual gross income. For example:

  • Annual income: $100,000 → Maximum borrowing: $600,000
  • Annual income: $150,000 → Maximum borrowing: $900,000

Note: This includes all debt (existing mortgages, personal loans, credit cards), not just your new home loan.

KiwiSaver for First Home Buyers

First-home buyers may be able to withdraw most of their KiwiSaver funds for a deposit:

  • Eligibility: Minimum 3 years membership in KiwiSaver
  • Withdrawal Amount: Can withdraw savings except the initial $1,000 government kickstart
  • Process: Apply through your KiwiSaver provider with signed Sale & Purchase Agreement

Note: The First Home Grant ended in May 2024 and is no longer available. However, the KiwiSaver withdrawal option and First Home Loan programme continue to operate.

Step 2: Get Mortgage Pre-Approval

Before house hunting, obtain mortgage pre-approval from a bank or through a mortgage broker:

  • Benefits: Know your borrowing limit, strengthen your negotiating position, speed up the purchasing process
  • Required Documents: Proof of income (payslips, tax returns), proof of deposit, identification, bank statements, details of debts and expenses
  • Timeline: Can typically be obtained within 1-3 business days
  • Validity: Usually valid for 90 days, subject to no material change in circumstances

Step 3: Budget for Additional Costs

Beyond the purchase price and deposit, budget for:

  • Legal Fees: $1,500 - $3,000+ for conveyancing services
  • LIM Report: $200 - $400 from local council
  • Building Inspection: $500 - $1,500+ depending on property size and complexity
  • Valuation: $500 - $1,000+ if required by lender
  • Insurance: Home and contents insurance (required before settlement)
  • Moving Costs: Removalists, truck rental, storage if needed
  • Council Rates: Pro-rated from settlement date
  • Chattels: Appliances, window treatments, garden equipment if not included

Step 4: Understand Due Diligence Requirements

LIM Report (Land Information Memorandum)

The LIM report is essential and contains:

  • Building consents and Code Compliance Certificates (CCCs)
  • Zoning information and permitted land uses
  • Natural hazard classifications (flooding, landslip, earthquake risk)
  • Services: water, wastewater, stormwater connections
  • Outstanding rates or charges
  • Council notices or requisitions

Building Inspection

Always commission a professional pre-purchase building inspection (NZS 4306:2005 standard):

  • Structural integrity and foundation condition
  • Weathertightness and moisture levels
  • Roof condition and expected life remaining
  • Plumbing and electrical systems
  • Drainage and ventilation
  • Evidence of pests (borer, rot)
  • Compliance observations

Title Search

Your lawyer will conduct a title search through LINZ (Land Information New Zealand) revealing:

  • Legal ownership confirmation
  • Title type (freehold, leasehold, cross-lease, unit title)
  • Easements, covenants, and encumbrances
  • Boundary information

Step 5: Making an Offer

Always include conditions in your offer:

  • Finance Condition: Subject to obtaining satisfactory mortgage approval
  • LIM Condition: Subject to review of LIM report
  • Building Inspection: Subject to satisfactory building report
  • Lawyer Approval: Subject to lawyer review of Sale & Purchase Agreement
  • Typical Timeframe: 10-15 working days for conditions to be satisfied or waived

Step 6: Settlement

The final stage where ownership transfers:

  • Typical settlement period: 4-6 weeks from unconditional agreement
  • Final pre-settlement inspection (verify property condition)
  • Insurance must be in place before settlement
  • Funds transferred, title registered, keys collected

Critical Warning: Never waive conditions without fully understanding the implications. If you discover problems after going unconditional, you typically cannot withdraw without significant financial penalties. When in doubt, extend condition dates rather than waiving them prematurely.

Complete Property Selling Guide for New Zealand

Updated November 2024: This guide reflects current New Zealand property law and vendor obligations under the Real Estate Agents Act 2008, Sale and Purchase of Land Act 2017, and related regulations.

Step 1: Prepare Your Property

Property Presentation

  • Declutter and Clean: Remove personal items, deep clean all areas, create neutral appeal
  • Minor Repairs: Fix leaky taps, squeaky doors, damaged fixtures, cracked tiles
  • Paint and Polish: Fresh paint in neutral colors significantly improves presentation
  • Gardens and Exterior: First impressions matter - mow lawns, trim hedges, clean paths and driveways
  • Professional Photography: Quality photos are essential for online listings
  • Consider Staging: Professional staging can increase perceived value and sale price

Vendor Due Diligence

Discuss with your property lawyer whether to conduct vendor due diligence to identify any issues requiring disclosure:

  • Building Consents: Verify all building work has proper consents
  • Code Compliance Certificates: Ensure all consented work has CCCs
  • Title Verification: Confirm title details are correct and up-to-date
  • Council Records: Check for notices, requisitions, or outstanding issues
  • Chattels List: Decide which items stay with the property

Step 2: Understand Your Legal Obligations

Disclosure Requirements

Vendors have legal obligations to disclose certain information:

  • Building Consent Issues: Must disclose unconsented work or missing CCCs
  • Known Defects: Any significant defects you're aware of (structural, weathertightness, etc.)
  • Natural Hazards: If you have knowledge of flooding, landslip, or earthquake damage
  • Legal Issues: Disputes, easements, encroachments affecting the property
  • Unit Title Properties: Body corporate records, levies, planned maintenance must be disclosed pre-contract

Legal Warning: Failing to disclose known issues can result in legal action, financial liability, or cancellation of the sale even after settlement. When in doubt, disclose. Consult your lawyer about disclosure obligations specific to your property.

Compliance Checks

  • Building Consents: Gather all building consent documentation
  • CCCs: Obtain certificates for all completed work
  • RMA Requirements: Section 225 Resource Management Act requirements for early deposit release
  • Certificates of Acceptance: If CCCs unavailable, may need retrospective COA from council

Step 3: Choose Your Selling Method

Auction

Advantages:

  • Creates urgency and competitive bidding
  • Can achieve premium prices in hot markets
  • Unconditional sale (buyers do due diligence beforehand)
  • Defined timeframe

Disadvantages:

  • May not reach reserve in slow markets
  • Higher marketing costs
  • Stressful for some vendors
  • Requires extensive pre-auction preparation

Fixed Price (By Negotiation)

Advantages:

  • Buyers know asking price upfront
  • Allows conditional offers (subject to finance, inspection)
  • More flexible negotiation process
  • Lower upfront marketing costs

Disadvantages:

  • May take longer to sell
  • Offers can fall through during conditional period
  • Risk of underpricing or overpricing

Tender

  • Sealed bids submitted by deadline
  • No price indication given
  • Vendor can choose best offer (not necessarily highest price)
  • Less common for residential properties

Step 4: Pricing Strategy

Work with your real estate agent to determine competitive pricing:

  • Comparative Market Analysis: Review recent sales of similar properties in your area
  • Current Market Conditions: Consider whether it's a buyer's or seller's market
  • Property Condition: Adjust for condition relative to comparables
  • Unique Features: Premium location, views, quality finishes, land size
  • Days on Market: Properties priced correctly typically sell within 30-60 days

Pricing Tip: Overpricing often results in longer time on market and eventually selling below a realistic price. Properties priced correctly from the start typically achieve better results and sell faster.

Step 5: Marketing Your Property

  • Online Listings: Trade Me Property, realestate.co.nz, agency websites
  • Professional Photography: Essential for online appeal
  • Signage: For Sale board with agent contact details
  • Open Homes: Scheduled viewings for multiple buyers
  • Social Media: Facebook, Instagram property promotion
  • Print Media: Local newspapers, property magazines (optional)
  • Email Marketing: Agent database of active buyers

Step 6: Managing Viewings

  • Keep property clean and tidy at all times
  • Be flexible with viewing times
  • Consider leaving during viewings (buyers speak more freely)
  • Maintain security - valuables secured, alarm system active
  • Pets managed appropriately
  • Let your agent manage communications

Step 7: Evaluating and Accepting Offers

Consider multiple factors beyond just price:

  • Offer Price: Highest isn't always best
  • Conditions: Fewer conditions = less risk of deal falling through
  • Finance Pre-Approval: Pre-approved buyers less likely to fail finance condition
  • Settlement Date: Does it align with your timing needs?
  • Deposit Amount: Higher deposits show commitment
  • Chattels: What's included/excluded

Step 8: From Sale to Settlement

  1. Conditional Period: 10-15 days typically for buyer to satisfy conditions
  2. Going Unconditional: Sale becomes legally binding
  3. Maintain Insurance: Keep property fully insured until settlement
  4. Maintain Property: Keep in same condition as at sale
  5. Pre-Settlement Inspection: Buyer conducts final check
  6. Settlement Day: Funds transferred, possession handed over

Tax Considerations

Potential tax obligations when selling property:

  • Bright-Line Test: Profit from residential property sold within 2 years (10 years if acquired after March 2021, or 5 years for new builds) may be taxable
  • Main Home Exemption: Your main home is typically exempt from bright-line tax
  • Investment Properties: May be subject to income tax on capital gains
  • GST: Generally not applicable to residential property unless you're a developer or in property business

Tax Advice: Tax rules are complex and depend on your specific circumstances. Always consult a qualified accountant or tax advisor before selling to understand your tax obligations.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Overpricing: Leads to extended time on market and lower final sale price
  • Poor Presentation: First impressions significantly impact buyer interest
  • Inadequate Disclosure: Can result in legal liability and deal cancellation
  • Emotional Attachment: Being too attached can cloud judgement on offers
  • Ignoring Agent Advice: Your agent knows the market - listen to their guidance
  • Refusing Reasonable Offers: Perfect offers are rare - consider all serious proposals
  • DIY Conveyancing: Property law is complex - always use a qualified solicitor
